On Bell's Inequality

Alice and Bob are the usual stand-ins for two distant measurement stations. A source sends them paired systems, each station chooses a measurement setting, and each station records a result that looks locally random.

Bell's theorem asks whether those paired results could have been explained by local prewritten answers. The answer is no: the observed correlation pattern can exceed the limit that any local hidden-variable story allows.

The puzzle in one sentence

Bob's measurement can look as if it instantly fixes Alice's distant result, but Bob cannot choose the result he gets, Alice's own log remains random, and the nonclassical part only appears when both logs are compared across several measurement settings.

Why Bell made this precise

Locality says that events here cannot be directly influenced by spacelike-separated events over there. Realism, in the hidden-variable sense, says measurement results reveal properties that were already definite. Ordinary causal independence says the later measurement choices were not secretly arranged by the source.

Bell showed that these assumptions are not merely philosophical preferences. Together they imply an upper bound on correlation. Quantum mechanics predicts, and experiments observe, correlations beyond that bound.
