Taxonomy

Bell arguments become slippery when words like measurement, randomness, correlation, locality, and hidden variables are used loosely. This section names the pieces before the classical and quantum animations use them.

Actors and records

Source
Prepares a paired system and sends one half to Alice and one half to Bob.
Alice and Bob
Distant measurement stations. Each chooses a setting and writes down a local result.
Setting
The measurement question asked at a station, shown in this module as two analyzer choices.
Outcome
The local result, simplified as plus or minus. Alone, each station's outcomes look random.

Assumptions under pressure

Locality
A result here is not directly changed by a distant setting or result chosen outside its light cone.
Prewritten answers
The classical hidden-variable picture: the pair carries answers for every possible future setting.
No signalling
Even quantum correlations cannot be used by Bob to send Alice a controllable message.
Measurement independence
The later setting choices are treated as not secretly coordinated with the source's hidden state.

Correlation

Correlation means the pattern in paired logs after Alice and Bob compare records. Assign plus the value +1 and minus the value -1. If a pair has the same result, the product is +1. If it has opposite results, the product is -1. Averaging those products for one setting pair gives its correlation.

  • +1: always same.
  • 0: no reliable same/opposite pattern.
  • -1: always opposite.
