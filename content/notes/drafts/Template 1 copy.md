## Example note title

Abductive reasoning in complex domains (that has stronger emergence, non-linearity, feedback, non-equilibrium) runs into three limits that feed on each other: the space of possible explanations is too large to search, the constraints that matter can't be broken into independent units and checked one at a time, and there's no general, efficient way to validate that a given explanation is good enough. Together these mean that no one—person or machine—can lay out every possible explanation, weigh them all, and verify the winner. So in practice abduction is never exhaustive; it's always a shortcut. You commit early to a handful of candidate explanations and reason from those.
What makes abduction strong isn't how many explanations you consider but what drives the choice. In strong abduction, candidates are picked and revised under explanatory pressure—tested against the problem as a whole, and dropped when a tempting local guess fails to fit the bigger picture, even if it looked good on its own. Large language models choose on a different footing. They go by plausibility: how likely a continuation is given what came before. That's a real shortcut, and often a good one, but it isn't a watered-down version of explanatory commitment—it's a different move altogether. Because plausibility doesn't answer to the global constraints that strong abduction has to satisfy, optimizing for it doesn't inch toward explanatory reasoning so much as sidestep it. That's why making a model more fluent doesn't, by itself, produce abduction in the strong sense.
