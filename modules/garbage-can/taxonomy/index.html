03 · The Garbage Can Model

Taxonomy

The Garbage Can Model classifies organisations along three structural dimensions. Their combination determines how problems, participants, and choice opportunities interact — and what kind of decision-making emerges.

The Three Dimensions

Energy Load

How much problem-energy the organisation carries relative to its capacity to process it. High load means more problems than the system can resolve in any given period. Low load means capacity exceeds demand.

Light: capacity exceeds demand. Problems get addressed. Moderate: roughly balanced. Some problems resolved, some deferred. Heavy: problems overwhelm capacity. Most decisions happen without resolving the underlying issue.

Decision Structure

Who gets to participate in which choice opportunities. This determines how energy (attention, effort) is distributed across forums.

Unsegmented: all decision makers can attend all forums. Energy spreads everywhere. Hierarchical: senior decision makers access more forums. Energy concentrates at the top. Specialized: each decision maker attends only their designated forum. Energy is siloed.

Access Structure

Which problems can reach which choice opportunities. This determines whether problems find forums where they could be resolved.

Unsegmented: any problem can surface in any forum. Problems float freely. Hierarchical: important problems access more forums. Lower-priority problems are restricted. Specialized: each problem is assigned to exactly one forum. No migration possible.

The Classification Grid

The combination of decision structure and access structure determines the organisation type. Energy load affects how severely the pattern manifests but does not change the type.

The Five Types

The Coherent Traditional Organisation

Hierarchical decision · Hierarchical access

The organisation is what it believes itself to be. Problems reach the forums designed for them. Decisions are made by the people with the authority to make them. The instruments of traditional management (governance structures, reporting lines, sanctioned decision forums) are reasonably well matched to the work being done. The Garbage Can Model has limited explanatory power here.

The Complexity-Informed Organisation

Unsegmented decision · Unsegmented access

The organisation has rejected the decomposable worldview, at least in practice. Problems surface wherever they can find a forum. Participation is fluid and expertise-driven rather than seniority-driven. This is not disorder — it is a different kind of order, one that the Garbage Can Model describes well. The risk is not anarchy but invisibility: when everything is permeable, it becomes difficult to know which decisions were actually made, by whom, and whether the underlying problem was resolved or merely displaced.

The Mix

Hierarchical decision · Unsegmented or Specialized access

The organisation has inherited the instruments of a decomposable worldview: governance structures, reporting lines, sanctioned decision forums. It applies them to a system that does not behave decomposably. Problems surface everywhere because the work itself is emergent. But decisions are made hierarchically, because the management frame requires it. The result is a permanent structural gap: problems live where the work is, decisions are made where the authority is, and the two rarely occupy the same room at the same time.

The Inverse Mix

Unsegmented or Specialized decision · Hierarchical or Specialized access

The organisation has open, fluid decision-making: in principle, anyone can weigh in. But the problems that reach decision forums are filtered before they arrive. Important issues that originate at the edges of the organisation travel slowly or not at all toward the forums where they could be addressed. The people in the room are willing to decide. The problems that most need deciding rarely make it through the door.

The Siloed Organisation

Specialized decision · Specialized access

The organisation has partitioned itself: problems are assigned to designated channels, people are assigned to designated forums. On paper this looks like clarity. In practice it means that problems which do not fit their designated channel have nowhere to go. The work that falls between the silos has no legitimate forum. It either disappears from view or creates informal decision-making that the organisation cannot see or govern.
