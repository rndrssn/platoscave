Utilization Trap

The team is not overloaded on average. It is only using about 72% of its capacity. Then the work arrives in lumps, some items turn out larger than expected, and the queue appears anyway.

That is the utilization trap: averages make the system look safe, while the lived experience is waiting, interruptions, stale work, and tomorrow's backlog.

The surprise is not that overloaded systems queue. Of course they do. The surprise is that a system can have enough average capacity and still feel overloaded because work does not arrive as an average.

Now give the story names

The controls above avoid notation on purpose. The math vocabulary is useful once the mechanism is visible: average work arriving is usually written as λ, average capacity as μ, utilization as ρ, lead time as W, and work in process as L.

Theoretical frame

Little's Law is the flow identity. In any stable system observed over a meaningful interval, average work in the system equals average throughput multiplied by average time in the system.

Little's Law does not say why waiting happens. It says the quantities must balance. If throughput is capped and WIP rises, lead time rises with it.

Assumptions

These equations describe stable average behavior, not a promise about any one item. The M/M/1 case assumes one server, random arrivals, random service times, and λ < μ. Kingman's G/G/1 approximation relaxes the arrival and service distributions but still assumes a stable single constrained service point.

Variable Mathematical meaning In high-software-content work Order-of-magnitude notes
L Average number of items in the system. Work in process: ideas, features, fixes, approvals, reviews, blocked work, and partially done delivery items. Can be counted in cards, tickets, decisions, or value-stream items. The unit must stay consistent.
λ Average arrival rate, equal to throughput rate in a stable system. Rate at which work enters the system, or rate at which value-bearing work exits once the system is stable. Usually measured per week or month. If arrivals exceed sustainable throughput, the system is not stable.
W Average time an item spends in the system. Lead time from accepted work to delivered or validated outcome. Often days to months. Use percentiles alongside averages because product work is skewed.
μ Average service rate of the constrained server. Rate at which the constrained team, role, approval, environment, or decision point can complete work. Can move when the constraint moves. In TOC terms the constraint may be policy, market, architecture, or authority, not only a person.
ρ Utilization, λ / μ. How close the constrained part of the system is to saturation. Near 1.0 the waiting-time curve becomes steep. High utilization is especially dangerous when arrivals and service are variable.
Ca Coefficient of variation of arrivals. Unevenness in demand: batching, urgent requests, quarterly planning waves, incidents, stakeholder interrupts. Can be below 1 for smoothed intake, around 1 for random arrivals, and above 1 when work arrives in bursts.
Cs Coefficient of variation of service times. Unevenness in work size and effort: discovery uncertainty, rework, reviews, integration, compliance, hidden dependencies. Often above 1 in high-software-content work because item size is fat-tailed and unknown work is common.
E[S] Expected service time. Average active processing time at the constraint, excluding waiting elsewhere. Must not be confused with lead time. Most elapsed time in overloaded systems is waiting, not service.

Why high utilization bites

The simplest queueing model is M/M/1: one server, random arrivals, random service times, and a stable arrival rate below service capacity. It is not a model of all product development. It is a deliberately small model that exposes the non-linearity.

This is the Reinertsen point made visible: high utilization of individual resources in a non-linear system does not merely remove slack. It creates queue buildup and amplifies variation.

Where variability enters

Product development work is rarely M/M/1. Arrivals are uneven, task sizes differ, rework appears, reviews block, and priorities change. Kingman's approximation for a G/G/1 queue separates the utilization effect from the variability effect.

The important part is the product of two forces: rho / (1 - rho) and (Ca^2 + Cs^2) / 2. Variability hurts at any utilization, but near saturation the system has no room to absorb it.

This is why high-software-content work can feel calm on paper and chaotic in practice. The average item may look manageable, while the tails do the damage: an urgent request batch, a hard integration, a slow review, or a dependency that turns one card into six.

Connection to Theory of Constraints

Theory of Constraints does not rest on Little's Law. It starts from a different claim: system throughput is governed by the constraint. In TOC, throughput is not generic activity. It is the rate at which the system generates value, traditionally money through sales.

Little's Law and queueing theory are complementary to TOC. They explain what happens when work is released faster than the constrained system can convert it into value: WIP rises, lead time rises, and variability is amplified. TOC supplies the management rule: identify the constraint, exploit it, subordinate other work to it, elevate it when needed, and repeat when the constraint moves.

TOC translation

TOC does not say the constraint should be idle. It says the constraint should be protected, exploited, and fed deliberately. The trap is different: optimizing every non-constraint resource for busyness releases work faster than the constraint can convert it into throughput.

The module's claim is therefore narrow: the goal is not to keep every person busy. The goal is to keep valuable work flowing through the constraint at a sustainable pace.

Reading notes

The argument borrows from three adjacent traditions: Little's Law for flow accounting, queueing theory for the utilization and variability curves, and Goldratt/Reinertsen-style flow management for the practical rule of protecting the constraint instead of maximizing local busyness.
