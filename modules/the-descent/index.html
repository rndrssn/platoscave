06 · The Descent
06.01 Stations

The Descent

Every piece of product work is a descent from an open problem to something verifiable. A good brief stages that descent rather than hiding it.

Sections should be ordered so ambiguity decreases as you read down. The top is for anyone; the bottom is where engineering lives. Written the other way around, design starts too late or locks in too early, and engineering gets asked to build things that were never actually decided.

What a brief is for

The brief is the artefact before convergence. It holds the problem open so design and engineering can explore. A PRD — or whatever the organisation calls its closing document — comes after, once the solution is understood.

Writing a PRD too early, before discovery has happened, is the most common failure mode. What looks like decisiveness is often just a refusal to hold ambiguity long enough for it to resolve into something worth committing to.

There are two kinds of briefs. Improvement briefs change something that already exists; they need a Where We Are section as an anchor, because design and engineering are not starting from zero. New-feature briefs have no such ground to stand on, so People has to carry more weight — it is the only scaffolding available.

The eight stations

Each section of the brief does one job. The further down you go, the less room there should be for interpretation.

1 · The Problem

Why this work exists at all. The anchor. Must be agreed before anything else moves. Ambiguity is highest here and that is correct — premature precision at the top of a brief is how solutions get smuggled in as problems.

2 · Direction

The desired change in the world. Outcome, not solution. This is the earliest place where design can start diverging, and the last place where the brief is still fully open. After this point the shape of the thing begins to matter.

3 · Where We Are (improvement briefs only)

A shared baseline — how the current thing works, what is broken, what the constraints of the existing system are. Without this, the first week of any improvement project is spent reconstructing it anyway.

4 · People

Who is affected, what they are trying to accomplish, under what conditions. Load-bearing in new-feature briefs where there is no existing feature to orient around. In improvement briefs this still matters, but Where We Are shares the weight.

5 · Scope

In, out, and open questions. The open-questions column is the one to watch: it should shrink as discovery progresses. A scope section that stays static is a scope section that is not being challenged.

6 · Slices (stories)

User-facing units of value. Written loosely here to keep exploration possible; tightened into ticket-ready form later, in the PRD or directly in the backlog. If you cannot yet write these, the direction above probably is not specific enough.

7 · Constraints

The boundaries the solution must operate within: legal, technical, deadlines, non-functional needs, integration dependencies. Should be short. If the list is long, scope decisions have crept in disguised as constraints.

8 · Acceptance Criteria

Binary pass/fail conditions. The most precise thing in the document, and the one with the least ambiguity. If these are hard to write, the sections above are not yet clear enough — which is information, not failure.

Three thresholds

A brief is not ready or not ready — it is ready for different things at different points in the descent. Conflating these thresholds is what causes design to either start too late or lock down too early.

Exploration opens (after Direction)

Problem and direction together are enough to start divergent thinking. You do not need users, scope, or stories yet. Waiting for them is how design shows up after the decisions are already made.

Design can commit (after Slices)

People, scope, and slices are all known. This is the right threshold for high-fidelity design work. Before this, detail is wasted; after this, open-endedness is expensive.

Build locks in (after Acceptance Criteria)

Fully unlocked only at acceptance criteria. Earlier sections contribute gradually; the bottom of the brief is where build can begin without interpretation.
